1. The Algebraic Engine of Root Flipping

Vieta Jumping acts as a highly specialized algebraic extension of the Infinite Descent method we covered in Module IV. By fixing all but one variable in a symmetric non-linear Diophantine equation, we map the relation to a polynomial (typically a quadratic). Vieta's formulas then mathematically guarantee the existence of a conjugate root, which allows us to "jump" from one valid coordinate to another within the solution space.

A quick structural note: In your initial request, you mentioned evaluating the condition $\frac{a^2+b^2}{ab-1} = k$ resulting in $k$ being a perfect square. It's worth briefly clarifying that the $ab-1$ denominator produces invariant integer sequences (for instance, $a=2, b=1$ yields $k=5$, which is not a square). The canonical formulation that strictly forces $k$ to be a perfect square relies on the $+1$ denominator. We will walk through this exact formulation below, as the root-flipping mechanics remain identical.

2. The Perfect Square Invariant (IMO 1988)

The defining application of Vieta Jumping establishes a rigid invariant for a specific rational algebraic fraction.

Theorem 5.1 (Perfect Square Invariant)

Let $a$ and $b$ be strictly positive integers such that $ab + 1$ divides $a^2 + b^2$. Then the resulting integer $k = \frac{a^2 + b^2}{ab + 1}$ must be a perfect square.

Assume for the sake of contradiction that there exist positive integers $a, b$ such that $\frac{a^2+b^2}{ab+1} = k$, but $k$ is not a perfect square. This sets up our descent.

Let $S = \{ (a, b) \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}^2 \mid a^2 + b^2 = k(ab + 1) \}$. By our assumption, $S$ is non-empty. By the Well-Ordering Principle, there must exist a minimal solution $(a, b) \in S$ such that the sum $a + b$ is strictly minimized. Due to the symmetry of the equation, we can assume without loss of generality that $a \ge b$.

We algebraically lock $b$ as a constant and view the relation as a monic quadratic equation in terms of a variable $x$:

$$x^2 - (kb)x + (b^2 - k) = 0$$

We already know one root of this equation is $x_1 = a$. Let the second root be $x_2$. By Vieta's formulas, the roots satisfy two critical constraints:

  1. $x_1 + x_2 = kb \implies x_2 = kb - a$
  2. $x_1 x_2 = b^2 - k \implies x_2 = \frac{b^2 - k}{a}$

From (1), since $k, b$, and $a$ are integers, $x_2$ is strictly an integer.

We now bound $x_2$ to show it constitutes a valid, smaller positive integer solution. First, we prove $x_2 \ge 0$. Suppose $x_2 < 0$. Because it is an integer, $x_2 \le -1$. Substituting this into our quadratic yields:

$$x_2^2 - kb x_2 + b^2 - k \ge 1 + kb + b^2 - k$$

Since $b \ge 1$ and $k$ is not a perfect square, $k \ge 2$, meaning $kb \ge k$, so the expression $1 + kb + b^2 - k > 0$. However, $x_2$ is a root, so the quadratic must equal exactly $0$. This contradiction strictly implies $x_2 \ge 0$.

If $x_2 = 0$, then from Vieta's product formula (2), $b^2 - k = a(0) = 0$, meaning $k = b^2$. This directly contradicts our initial assumption that $k$ is not a perfect square! Thus, $x_2$ must be strictly positive: $x_2 > 0$.

Finally, we check the magnitude of $x_2$ against $a$ using Vieta's product formula (2):

$$x_2 = \frac{b^2 - k}{a} < \frac{b^2}{a} \le \frac{a^2}{a} = a$$

We have successfully constructed a new pair $(x_2, b)$ consisting of strictly positive integers that satisfies the original equation. But wait:

$$x_2 + b < a + b$$

This strictly violates our assumption that $(a, b)$ was the minimal solution in $S$. Therefore, the initial assumption must be false. The integer $k$ must inherently be a perfect square.

$\blacksquare$

3. Algorithmic Realization: Upward Jumping
