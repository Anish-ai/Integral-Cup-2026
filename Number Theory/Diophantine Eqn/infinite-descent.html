Module IV: The Method of Infinite Descent

Algorithmic Number Theory & Abstract Algebra • Advanced Undergraduate Sequence

1. Well-Ordering and the Descent Principle

The Method of Infinite Descent, formalized by Pierre de Fermat, is a highly specialized proof by contradiction relying on the topological discrete nature of $\mathbb{N}$. It acts as the contrapositive to mathematical induction.

Definition 4.1 (Well-Ordering Principle)

Every non-empty subset of the non-negative integers has a least element. Consequently, there can exist no strictly decreasing infinite sequence of non-negative integers: $a_1 > a_2 > a_3 > \dots \ge 0$.

To prove that a Diophantine equation possesses no non-trivial solutions via infinite descent, one assumes a solution exists. By the Well-Ordering Principle, there must exist a "minimal" solution with respect to some positive integer parameter (often the sum or maximum of the variables). Algebraic manipulation of this minimal solution constructs a strictly smaller integer solution, violating the assumption of minimality. Thus, the original set of solutions must be empty.

2. Fermat's Right Triangle Theorem (FLT for n = 4)

The most celebrated application of infinite descent establishes the impossibility of a Pythagorean triangle possessing an area equal to the square of an integer. This identically proves Fermat's Last Theorem for the exponent $n=4$.

Theorem 4.2 (Fermat's Last Theorem, $n=4$)

The Diophantine equation $x^4 + y^4 = z^2$ has no solutions in strictly positive integers $x, y, z$.

Assume for the sake of contradiction that solutions exist. Let $(x, y, z)$ be a solution such that $z > 0$ is minimal. Without loss of generality, assume $\gcd(x, y) = 1$, as any common factor could be divided out to yield a smaller $z$.

We rewrite the equation as $(x^2)^2 + (y^2)^2 = z^2$. This forms a primitive Pythagorean triple. By Theorem 2.2, one of $x^2$ or $y^2$ is even. Suppose $x^2$ is even (so $x$ is even, and $y, z$ are odd). There exist coprime integers $m > n > 0$ of opposite parity such that:

$$x^2 = 2mn, \quad y^2 = m^2 - n^2, \quad z = m^2 + n^2$$

Consider the equation $y^2 = m^2 - n^2 \implies y^2 + n^2 = m^2$. This is another primitive Pythagorean triple since $\gcd(m,n)=1$. Because $y$ is odd, $n$ must be even and $m$ must be odd. Applying Theorem 2.2 again, there exist coprime integers $r > s > 0$ of opposite parity such that:

$$n = 2rs, \quad y = r^2 - s^2, \quad m = r^2 + s^2$$

Substitute these parametrizations of $m$ and $n$ back into the expression for $x^2$:

$$x^2 = 2mn = 2(r^2 + s^2)(2rs) = 4rs(r^2 + s^2) \implies \left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^2 = rs(r^2 + s^2)$$

Since $\gcd(r, s) = 1$, it follows that $r, s$, and $r^2 + s^2$ are pairwise coprime. Their product is a perfect square, which requires that each individual factor must be a perfect square. Therefore, there exist positive integers $u, v, w$ such that:

$$r = u^2, \quad s = v^2, \quad r^2 + s^2 = w^2$$

Substituting $r$ and $s$ into the third equation yields:

$$u^4 + v^4 = w^2$$

We have constructed a new solution $(u, v, w)$ to the original Diophantine equation. We now check the minimality condition on $w$:

$$w \le w^2 = r^2 + s^2 = m \le m^2 < m^2 + n^2 = z$$

Thus, $0 < w < z$. We have generated a strictly positive solution with a strictly smaller $z$, contradicting our initial assumption that $z$ was minimal. By the Principle of Infinite Descent, no such positive integer solutions can exist.

$\blacksquare$
