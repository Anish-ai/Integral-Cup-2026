1. Primitive Pythagorean Triples

We transition into non-linear analysis by classifying the integer solutions to the canonical quadratic Diophantine equation $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$. A triple $(x, y, z) \in \mathbb{Z}^3_{>0}$ is termed a Primitive Pythagorean Triple (PPT) if $\gcd(x, y, z) = 1$.

Lemma 2.1 (Parity Constraints)

If $(x,y,z)$ is a PPT, then exactly one of $x$ or $y$ is even, and $z$ is odd.

If both $x$ and $y$ are even, then $2 \mid x$ and $2 \mid y \implies 4 \mid (x^2 + y^2) = z^2 \implies 2 \mid z$. This contradicts $\gcd(x,y,z)=1$.

If both $x$ and $y$ are odd, then $x^2 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ and $y^2 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. Thus, $z^2 = x^2 + y^2 \equiv 2 \pmod 4$. However, quadratic residues modulo 4 can only be 0 or 1. Thus, $x$ and $y$ must have opposite parity. Consequently, $z^2 = \text{odd} + \text{even} = \text{odd}$, rendering $z$ odd.

$\blacksquare$
Theorem 2.2 (Euclid's Formula)

Every primitive Pythagorean triple $(x, y, z)$ with $x$ even is parameterized by exactly one pair of relatively prime integers $m, n > 0$ of opposite parity, such that $m > n$, given by:

$$x = 2mn, \quad y = m^2 - n^2, \quad z = m^2 + n^2$$

From Lemma 2.1, $y$ and $z$ are both odd. We algebraically manipulate the equation $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$:

$$x^2 = z^2 - y^2 = (z - y)(z + y) \implies \left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{z - y}{2}\right)\left(\frac{z + y}{2}\right)$$

Since $y$ and $z$ are odd, $\frac{z-y}{2}$ and $\frac{z+y}{2}$ are integers. Let $d = \gcd\left(\frac{z-y}{2}, \frac{z+y}{2}\right)$. Then $d$ divides their sum $(z)$ and their difference $(y)$. Since $\gcd(y, z) = 1$ (from the PPT assumption), we conclude $d = 1$.

The product of two coprime positive integers is a perfect square if and only if each is a perfect square. Thus, there exist integers $m, n > 0$ such that:

$$\frac{z+y}{2} = m^2 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{z-y}{2} = n^2$$

Solving this linear system for $y$ and $z$ yields $z = m^2 + n^2$ and $y = m^2 - n^2$. Substituting these back reveals $x = 2mn$. Coprimality of $m,n$ follows from $\gcd(y,z)=1$. Opposite parity of $m,n$ is mandated because if both were odd, $y$ and $z$ would be even, violating the PPT condition.

$\blacksquare$

2. Fermat's Theorem on Sums of Two Squares

We investigate the representation of integers by the quadratic form $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$. A fundamental result in number theory determines exactly which primes can be expressed in this manner.

Lemma 2.3 (Thue's Lemma)

Let $n$ be an integer $>1$ and let $a$ be an integer such that $\gcd(a, n) = 1$. There exist integers $x$ and $y$ satisfying $0 < |x| \le \lfloor\sqrt{n}\rfloor$ and $0 < |y| \le \lfloor\sqrt{n}\rfloor$ such that $ax \equiv y \pmod n$.

Theorem 2.4 (Fermat, 1640)

An odd prime $p$ can be expressed as the sum of two integer squares, $p = x^2 + y^2$, if and only if $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. This representation is uniquely determined up to sign and order.

($\implies$) Suppose $p = x^2 + y^2$. Since $p$ is odd, $\gcd(x,p) = \gcd(y,p) = 1$. We have $x^2 \equiv -y^2 \pmod p$. Multiplying by $(y^{-1})^2$, we obtain $(x y^{-1})^2 \equiv -1 \pmod p$. By Euler's Criterion, $\left(\frac{-1}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = 1$, which necessitates $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$.

($\impliedby$) Assume $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. By Euler's Criterion, $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$. Hence, there exists an integer $a$ such that $a^2 \equiv -1 \pmod p$.

Apply Thue's Lemma (Lemma 2.3) to $a$ and $p$. There exist integers $x, y \in (0, \lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor]$ such that $ax \equiv y \pmod p$.

Squaring this congruence yields:

$$y^2 \equiv a^2x^2 \equiv -x^2 \pmod p \implies x^2 + y^2 \equiv 0 \pmod p$$

Since $x, y \le \lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor < \sqrt{p}$, we strictly bound the sum: $0 < x^2 + y^2 < (\sqrt{p})^2 + (\sqrt{p})^2 = 2p$.

The only multiple of $p$ strictly between $0$ and $2p$ is $p$ itself. Therefore, we rigidly conclude $x^2 + y^2 = p$.

$\blacksquare$

3. Lagrange's Four-Square Theorem

While the subset of primes representable by two squares is strictly governed by modulo 4 conditions, extending the quadratic form to four variables envelops the entirety of $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$.

Theorem 2.5 (Lagrange, 1770)

Every non-negative integer can be expressed as the sum of four integer squares: $n = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2$.

The proof structurally relies on Euler's Four-Square Identity, which establishes that the set of sums of four squares is closed under multiplication:

$$(a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2 + a_4^2)(b_1^2 + b_2^2 + b_3^2 + b_4^2) = $$ $$(a_1b_1 - a_2b_2 - a_3b_3 - a_4b_4)^2 + (a_1b_2 + a_2b_1 + a_3b_4 - a_4b_3)^2 + $$ $$(a_1b_3 - a_2b_4 + a_3b_1 + a_4b_2)^2 + (a_1b_4 + a_2b_3 - a_3b_2 + a_4b_1)^2$$

Due to this multiplicative closure, and noting $1=1^2+0^2+0^2+0^2$ and $2=1^2+1^2+0^2+0^2$, it suffices to prove the theorem for odd primes $p$.

By the Pigeonhole Principle on the finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$, the sets $A = \{a^2 \pmod p \mid 0 \le a \le (p-1)/2\}$ and $B = \{-b^2 - 1 \pmod p \mid 0 \le b \le (p-1)/2\}$ both possess $(p+1)/2$ elements. Since their sum $|A| + |B| = p + 1 > p$, they must intersect. Thus, there exist $a, b$ such that $a^2 \equiv -b^2 - 1 \pmod p$, or $a^2 + b^2 + 1^2 + 0^2 = mp$ for some $1 \le m < p$.

The proof proceeds via infinite descent: Assuming $mp$ is a sum of four squares, one algebraically manipulates the representation to construct a strictly smaller multiple $m'p$ (where $1 \le m' < m$) that is also a sum of four squares. This descent invariably terminates at $m=1$, demonstrating $p$ is a sum of four squares.

$\blacksquare$
