Problem 1: Let $p$ be an odd prime. Prove that the product of all primitive roots modulo $p$ is congruent to $1 \pmod p$ if $p > 3$, and $-1 \pmod p$ if $p = 3$.
Solution. Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. All primitive roots are of the form $g^k$ where $1 \le k \le p-1$ and $\gcd(k, p-1) = 1$.
The product of all primitive roots is: $$ P \equiv \prod_{\gcd(k, p-1)=1} g^k \equiv g^{\sum k} \pmod p $$ Let $S = \sum_{\gcd(k, p-1)=1} k$. If $\gcd(k, p-1) = 1$, then $\gcd(p-1-k, p-1) = 1$.
Thus, we can pair each $k$ with $p-1-k$. For $p > 3$, $\phi(p-1)$ is even and greater than 1. There are $\phi(p-1)/2$ such pairs, each summing to $p-1$.
$$ S = \frac{\phi(p-1)}{2}(p-1) $$ Since $p-1$ is even, $S$ is a multiple of $p-1$. By Fermat's Little Theorem, $g^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$. Therefore: $$ P \equiv g^{c(p-1)} \equiv 1 \pmod p \quad \text{for } p > 3 $$ For $p = 3$, the only primitive root is $2$, and its product is $2 \equiv -1 \pmod 3$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 2: Solve the non-linear congruence $x^{11} \equiv 2 \pmod{17}$.
Solution. First, find a primitive root modulo 17. Test $g=3$:
$\phi(17) = 16$. Prime factors of 16 are 2.
$3^{16/2} = 3^8 = (3^4)^2 \equiv 81^2 \equiv (-4)^2 \equiv 16 \equiv -1 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{17}$. Thus, $g=3$ is a primitive root.
Construct the index (discrete log) to base 3: Take index of both sides: $\text{ind}_3(x^{11}) \equiv \text{ind}_3(2) \pmod{16}$.
Let's find $\text{ind}_3(2)$: $3^1=3, 3^2=9, 3^3=10, 3^4=13, 3^5=5, 3^6=15, 3^7=11, 3^8=16, 3^9=14, 3^{10}=8, 3^{11}=7, 3^{12}=4, 3^{13}=12, 3^{14}=2$.
So, $\text{ind}_3(2) = 14$.
The congruence becomes: $11 \cdot \text{ind}_3(x) \equiv 14 \pmod{16}$.
We need the modular inverse of 11 modulo 16. Since $11 \equiv -5$, we find $-5 \cdot y \equiv 1 \pmod{16} \implies y = 3$.
Multiply by 3: $33 \cdot \text{ind}_3(x) \equiv 42 \pmod{16} \implies 1 \cdot \text{ind}_3(x) \equiv 10 \pmod{16}$.
We need $x \equiv 3^{10} \pmod{17}$. From our table, $3^{10} = 8$.
Thus, $x \equiv 8 \pmod{17}$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 3: Prove rigorously that the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is not cyclic for $k \ge 3$, implying no primitive roots exist.
Solution. To show no primitive root exists, we prove that the maximal order of any odd integer $a$ modulo $2^k$ is strictly less than $\phi(2^k) = 2^{k-1}$.
Specifically, we use induction to prove that for any odd integer $a$, $a^{2^{k-2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$ for $k \ge 3$.
Base Case ($k=3$): $\phi(8) = 4$. For $a \in \{1, 3, 5, 7\}$, $a^2 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$. Thus $a^{2^{3-2}} = a^2 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$. True.
Inductive Step: Assume $a^{2^{k-2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$ for some $k \ge 3$.
This means $a^{2^{k-2}} = 1 + c \cdot 2^k$ for some integer $c$.
Squaring both sides:
$$(a^{2^{k-2}})^2 = (1 + c \cdot 2^k)^2$$ $$a^{2^{k-1}} = 1 + c \cdot 2^{k+1} + c^2 \cdot 2^{2k}$$ Since $k \ge 3$, $2k \ge k+1$. Thus, $c^2 \cdot 2^{2k}$ is divisible by $2^{k+1}$.
Therefore, $a^{2^{k-1}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k+1}}$.
By induction, this holds for all $k \ge 3$.
Since the order of any element divides $2^{k-2}$, the maximal order is at most $2^{k-2}$, which is strictly less than the group order $2^{k-1}$. Thus, the group is not cyclic. $\blacksquare$
Problem 4: Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo an odd prime $p$. Prove that $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$ if and only if $g^{p-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$.
Solution. $(\Rightarrow)$ If $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$, its order modulo $p^2$ is $\phi(p^2) = p(p-1)$. If $g^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$, its order would divide $p-1$, contradicting that it generates $(\mathbb{Z}/p^2\mathbb{Z})^\times$. Thus, $g^{p-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Assume $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p$ and $g^{p-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$.
Let $h$ be the order of $g$ modulo $p^2$. By Euler's theorem, $h \mid \phi(p^2)$, so $h \mid p(p-1)$.
Since $g^h \equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$, it implies $g^h \equiv 1 \pmod p$. Because $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p$, we must have $(p-1) \mid h$.
Since $(p-1) \mid h$ and $h \mid p(p-1)$, $h$ must be either $p-1$ or $p(p-1)$.
By our hypothesis, $g^{p-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$, so $h \neq p-1$.
Therefore, $h = p(p-1) = \phi(p^2)$, proving $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 5: Let $\text{ord}_n(a) = h$. Prove that the order of $a^k$ modulo $n$ is $\frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)}$.
Solution. Let $r = \text{ord}_n(a^k)$. We want to show $r = \frac{h}{\gcd(h,k)}$.
By definition, $(a^k)^r \equiv 1 \pmod n \implies a^{kr} \equiv 1 \pmod n$.
Since the order of $a$ is $h$, we must have $h \mid kr$.
Divide both sides of the divisibility relation by $\gcd(h, k)$:
$$ \frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)} \;\Big|\; \frac{k}{\gcd(h, k)} \cdot r $$ Since $\frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)}$ and $\frac{k}{\gcd(h, k)}$ are coprime, it follows by Euclid's Lemma that:
$$ \frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)} \;\Big|\; r $$ Conversely, consider the exponent $\frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)}$:
$$ (a^k)^{\frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)}} = a^{\frac{kh}{\gcd(h, k)}} = (a^h)^{\frac{k}{\gcd(h, k)}} \equiv 1^{\frac{k}{\gcd(h, k)}} \equiv 1 \pmod n $$ Since $r$ is the smallest positive exponent yielding 1, $r \le \frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)}$.
Combined with the divisibility condition, $r = \frac{h}{\gcd(h, k)}$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 6: Determine the number of incongruent solutions to $x^3 \equiv 1 \pmod p$ for a prime $p > 3$. Separate the analysis based on the modular class of $p \pmod 3$.
Solution. Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. We map the congruence to the index space:
$$ \text{ind}_g(x^3) \equiv \text{ind}_g(1) \pmod{p-1} \implies 3 \cdot \text{ind}_g(x) \equiv 0 \pmod{p-1} $$ This is a linear congruence of the form $Ay \equiv B \pmod M$, which has $\gcd(A, M)$ solutions if $\gcd(A, M) \mid B$.
Here, $A = 3$, $B = 0$, $M = p-1$. Since $\gcd(3, p-1)$ always divides $0$, the number of solutions is exactly $\gcd(3, p-1)$.

Case 1: $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$
Then $p-1$ is a multiple of 3, so $\gcd(3, p-1) = 3$. There are exactly 3 solutions.
Case 2: $p \equiv 2 \pmod 3$
Then $p-1$ is not a multiple of 3. Since 3 is prime, $\gcd(3, p-1) = 1$. There is exactly 1 solution (which is trivially $x=1$). $\blacksquare$
Problem 7: Prove that the sum of the primitive roots modulo a prime $p$ is congruent to $\mu(p-1) \pmod p$, where $\mu$ is the Möbius function.
Solution. Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. The primitive roots are $g^k$ for $1 \le k \le p-1$ with $\gcd(k, p-1) = 1$.
We are evaluating $S = \sum_{\gcd(k, p-1)=1} g^k \pmod p$.
Using the property of the Möbius function $\sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d) = 1$ if $n=1$, and $0$ otherwise:
$$ S = \sum_{k=1}^{p-1} g^k \sum_{d \mid \gcd(k, p-1)} \mu(d) = \sum_{d \mid p-1} \mu(d) \sum_{m=1}^{(p-1)/d} g^{md} $$ Consider the inner sum $T(d) = \sum_{m=1}^{(p-1)/d} (g^d)^m$. This is a geometric series.
If $d = p-1$, then $g^d \equiv 1 \pmod p$, so $T(p-1) = 1$.
If $d < p-1$, then $g^d \not\equiv 1 \pmod p$. The sum of a full geometric progression of roots of unity evaluates to: $$ T(d) = g^d \frac{(g^d)^{(p-1)/d} - 1}{g^d - 1} = g^d \frac{g^{p-1} - 1}{g^d - 1} \equiv 0 \pmod p $$ Thus, the only non-zero term in the outer sum occurs when $d = p-1$.
$$ S \equiv \mu(p-1) \cdot T(p-1) \equiv \mu(p-1) \cdot 1 \equiv \mu(p-1) \pmod p $$ $\blacksquare$
Problem 8: Implement a highly optimized C++ function w(A, B, M, g) to solve $x^A \equiv B \pmod M$ using the Baby-step Giant-step algorithm, given a known primitive root $g$. Do not include comments and use single-letter variables.
Solution. To solve $x^A \equiv B \pmod M$, we first compute $k = \text{ind}_g(B)$ using BSGS. Then we solve $A \cdot y \equiv k \pmod{M-1}$ using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm, returning $g^y \pmod M$. 
#include <map>
#include <cmath>

long long p(long long b, long long e, long long m) {
    long long r = 1; b %= m;
    while (e) {
        if (e & 1) r = (r * b) % m;
        b = (b * b) % m; e >>= 1;
    }
    return r;
}

long long d(long long a, long long b, long long& x, long long& y) {
    if (b == 0) { x = 1; y = 0; return a; }
    long long x1, y1;
    long long g = d(b, a % b, x1, y1);
    x = y1; y = x1 - y1 * (a / b);
    return g;
}

long long s(long long g, long long h, long long m) {
    long long n = std::sqrt(m) + 1;
    std::map<long long, long long> t;
    long long c = h % m;
    for (long long j = 0; j < n; ++j) {
        t[c] = j; c = (c * g) % m;
    }
    long long f = p(g, n, m), v = f;
    for (long long i = 1; i <= n; ++i) {
        if (t.count(v)) return i * n - t[v];
        v = (v * f) % m;
    }
    return -1;
}

long long w(long long A, long long B, long long M, long long g) {
    long long k = s(g, B, M);
    if (k == -1) return -1;
    long long x, y;
    long long u = d(A, M - 1, x, y);
    if (k % u != 0) return -1;
    long long l = (M - 1) / u;
    long long y0 = ((x * (k / u)) % l + l) % l;
    return p(g, y0, M);
}
Problem 9: Let $p$ be an odd prime. Prove using discrete logarithms that $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$ if and only if $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$.
Solution. Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. We are solving $x^2 \equiv -1 \pmod p$.
By Fermat's Little Theorem, $g^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$. Since $g^{(p-1)/2}$ is a square root of 1 but cannot be 1 (as $g$ is primitive), it must be that $g^{(p-1)/2} \equiv -1 \pmod p$.
Thus, $\text{ind}_g(-1) = \frac{p-1}{2}$.
Taking the discrete log of the equation $x^2 \equiv -1 \pmod p$ yields: $$ 2 \cdot \text{ind}_g(x) \equiv \frac{p-1}{2} \pmod{p-1} $$ This linear congruence has a solution if and only if $\gcd(2, p-1)$ divides $\frac{p-1}{2}$.
Since $p$ is an odd prime, $\gcd(2, p-1) = 2$. Thus, we require $2 \mid \frac{p-1}{2}$.
This holds true if and only if $\frac{p-1}{2}$ is an even integer, which means $\frac{p-1}{2} = 2k \implies p - 1 = 4k \implies p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 10: Use index arithmetic to prove Wilson's Theorem: $(p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$ for any odd prime $p$.
Solution. Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. The elements $1, 2, \dots, p-1$ correspond to the powers $g^1, g^2, \dots, g^{p-1}$ in some permuted order.
The factorial $(p-1)!$ is the product of all these elements: $$ (p-1)! = \prod_{k=1}^{p-1} g^k = g^{\sum_{k=1}^{p-1} k} $$ Evaluate the exponent sum: $$ \sum_{k=1}^{p-1} k = \frac{(p-1)p}{2} $$ So, $(p-1)! \equiv g^{\frac{p(p-1)}{2}} \pmod p$.
Rewrite the exponent as $\frac{p-1}{2} \cdot p$. Thus: $$ (p-1)! \equiv (g^{\frac{p-1}{2}})^p \pmod p $$ As proven in Problem 9, for a primitive root $g$, $g^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \equiv -1 \pmod p$.
Substituting this in: $$ (p-1)! \equiv (-1)^p \pmod p $$ Since $p$ is an odd prime, $(-1)^p = -1$. Therefore, $(p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 11: Let $p = 2q + 1$ where $p$ and $q$ are both odd primes. Show that an integer $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p$ if and only if $g \not\equiv \pm 1 \pmod p$ and $g^q \not\equiv 1 \pmod p$.
Solution. The order of any element modulo $p$ must divide $\phi(p) = p-1 = 2q$.
Since $q$ is prime, the divisors of $2q$ are $1, 2, q,$ and $2q$.
An element $g$ is a primitive root if and only if its order is exactly $2q$. This means its order cannot be $1, 2,$ or $q$.
- If $\text{ord}_p(g) = 1$, then $g^1 \equiv 1 \pmod p$, so $g \equiv 1 \pmod p$.
- If $\text{ord}_p(g) = 2$, then $g^2 \equiv 1 \pmod p$, which implies $g \equiv -1 \pmod p$ (since $g \neq 1$).
- If $\text{ord}_p(g) = q$, then $g^q \equiv 1 \pmod p$.
Therefore, excluding orders 1, 2, and $q$ means verifying $g \not\equiv 1$, $g \not\equiv -1$, and $g^q \not\equiv 1$.
If all three conditions hold, the order must be $2q$, making $g$ a primitive root. $\blacksquare$
Problem 12: Given that $2$ is a primitive root modulo $5$, deduce a primitive root modulo $25$ using lifting, proving the steps mathematically.
Solution. By Theorem (Primitive Roots mod $p^2$), if $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p$, then $g$ or $g+p$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$.
We are given $p=5$ and $g=2$. We evaluate $g^{p-1} \pmod{p^2}$, which is $2^4 \pmod{25}$.
$2^4 = 16$. Since $16 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{25}$, the condition $g^{p-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$ holds directly for $g=2$.
Thus, $2$ is already a primitive root modulo $25$.
To verify strictly: $\phi(25) = 20$. Divisors are $1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20$. We check maximal prime quotients $20/2 = 10$ and $20/5 = 4$.
$2^{10} = 1024 \equiv 24 \equiv -1 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{25}$.
$2^4 = 16 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{25}$.
Hence, order is 20. $g=2$ is primitive. $\blacksquare$
Problem 13: Solve the exponential congruence $3^x \equiv x \pmod 7$.
Solution. This requires solving for $x$ in both the base and the exponent. We note that modulo 7, the base operates in $\mathbb{Z}_7$ while the exponent operates modulo $\phi(7) = 6$.
We must find an $x$ such that $x \pmod 7$ and $x \pmod 6$ satisfy the relation.
By Chinese Remainder Theorem, $x$ cycles every $\text{lcm}(7, 6) = 42$. We can test the possible values of $x \pmod 7$.
Let's evaluate $3^x \pmod 7$ for $x \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$:
$x \equiv 0 \pmod 6 \implies 3^0 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. We need $x \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. Is there $x$ such that $x \equiv 0 \pmod 6$ and $x \equiv 1 \pmod 7$? Yes, $x=36$.
$x \equiv 1 \pmod 6 \implies 3^1 \equiv 3 \pmod 7$. We need $x \equiv 3 \pmod 7$. Check CRT: $x \equiv 1 \pmod 6$ and $x \equiv 3 \pmod 7 \implies x=31$.
$x \equiv 2 \pmod 6 \implies 3^2 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$. Check CRT: $x \equiv 2 \pmod 6$ and $x \equiv 2 \pmod 7 \implies x=2$.
$x \equiv 3 \pmod 6 \implies 3^3 \equiv 6 \pmod 7$. Check CRT: $x \equiv 3 \pmod 6$ and $x \equiv 6 \pmod 7 \implies x=27$.
$x \equiv 4 \pmod 6 \implies 3^4 \equiv 4 \pmod 7$. Check CRT: $x \equiv 4 \pmod 6$ and $x \equiv 4 \pmod 7 \implies x=4$.
$x \equiv 5 \pmod 6 \implies 3^5 \equiv 5 \pmod 7$. Check CRT: $x \equiv 5 \pmod 6$ and $x \equiv 5 \pmod 7 \implies x=5$.
Thus, the solutions are $x \equiv 2, 4, 5, 27, 31, 36 \pmod{42}$. $\blacksquare$
Problem 14: Prove that if $a$ is a primitive root modulo $p$, then $a$ cannot be a quadratic residue modulo $p$.
Solution. Assume for contradiction that $a$ is both a primitive root and a quadratic residue modulo $p$.
Since $a$ is a quadratic residue, by Euler's Criterion: $$ a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv 1 \pmod p $$ However, by the definition of a primitive root, the order of $a$ modulo $p$ must be exactly $p-1$.
This implies that no positive integer $k < p-1$ can satisfy $a^k \equiv 1 \pmod p$.
Since $(p-1)/2 < p-1$, $a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv 1 \pmod p$ directly contradicts the minimality of the order $p-1$.
Therefore, $a$ cannot be a quadratic residue. $\blacksquare$
Problem 15: Explain conceptually why Pollard's $\rho$ algorithm requires $O(\sqrt{m})$ time using the Birthday Paradox.
Solution. Pollard's $\rho$ algorithm generates a pseudo-random sequence $x_i \in (\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z})^\times$. The group has size $\phi(m) \approx m$.
By the Birthday Paradox, if elements are drawn uniformly at random from a set of size $N$, the expected number of draws before a collision (i.e., a repeated element) occurs is approximately $\sqrt{\pi N / 2}$.
Applying this to our sequence where $N \approx m$, a collision $x_i = x_j$ is expected to occur after $O(\sqrt{m})$ steps.
Since the sequence is generated deterministically ($x_{k+1} = f(x_k)$), once a collision occurs, the sequence enters a cycle (the shape of the Greek letter $\rho$).
Floyd's cycle-finding algorithm detects this cycle in time proportional to the cycle length, which is bounded by the collision time $O(\sqrt{m})$.
This yields the discrete logarithm in $O(\sqrt{m})$ time, requiring only $O(1)$ space to store the tortoise and hare pointers. $\blacksquare$
