C. Discrete Logarithms (Indices)

Once we establish that a multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ possesses a primitive root $g$, every element $a$ coprime to $n$ can be expressed as a unique power of $g$. This observation allows us to transform complex multiplicative congruences into simpler additive ones, directly mirroring how standard logarithms map multiplication to addition over the real numbers.

Definition 4: Discrete Logarithm (Index) Let $n$ be an integer having a primitive root $g$. If $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, the smallest non-negative integer $k$ such that: $$g^k \equiv a \pmod n$$ is called the discrete logarithm or index of $a$ to the base $g$ modulo $n$. It is denoted as $\text{ind}_g(a)$ or $\log_g(a)$.

Because the order of the primitive root $g$ is exactly $\phi(n)$, the exponents operate modulo $\phi(n)$. This fundamental property leads to the following crucial isomorphism.

Theorem 3.1: Group Isomorphism Let $n$ have a primitive root $g$. The mapping $f: (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times \to (\mathbb{Z}/\phi(n)\mathbb{Z})^+$ defined by: $$f(a) = \text{ind}_g(a)$$ is a group isomorphism between the multiplicative group of integers modulo $n$ and the additive group of integers modulo $\phi(n)$.
Proof.
To prove $f$ is an isomorphism, we must show it is a bijection and preserves the group operation (a homomorphism).

Bijection: Since $g$ is a primitive root, its powers $g^0, g^1, \dots, g^{\phi(n)-1}$ generate all $\phi(n)$ distinct elements of $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$. Thus, every $a$ has exactly one index in the range $[0, \phi(n)-1]$, making $f$ both injective and surjective.

Homomorphism: Let $a, b \in (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$. Suppose $\text{ind}_g(a) = k_1$ and $\text{ind}_g(b) = k_2$. Then $a \equiv g^{k_1} \pmod n$ and $b \equiv g^{k_2} \pmod n$.
Multiplying them gives: $$ab \equiv g^{k_1} \cdot g^{k_2} \equiv g^{k_1 + k_2} \pmod n$$ Taking the index of both sides: $$\text{ind}_g(ab) \equiv k_1 + k_2 \pmod{\phi(n)}$$ Substituting the indices back: $$\text{ind}_g(ab) \equiv \text{ind}_g(a) + \text{ind}_g(b) \pmod{\phi(n)}$$ This confirms that multiplication in the domain maps exactly to addition modulo $\phi(n)$ in the codomain. $\blacksquare$

From this theorem, we immediately derive the rules of index arithmetic. For any integer $k \ge 0$:

  • $\text{ind}_g(1) \equiv 0 \pmod{\phi(n)}$
  • $\text{ind}_g(ab) \equiv \text{ind}_g(a) + \text{ind}_g(b) \pmod{\phi(n)}$
  • $\text{ind}_g(a^k) \equiv k \cdot \text{ind}_g(a) \pmod{\phi(n)}$

Solving Congruences Using Indices

The true power of discrete logarithms lies in transforming difficult non-linear congruences into simple linear Diophantine equations. Let us analyze the congruence $x^k \equiv a \pmod n$.

Example: Solving Non-Linear Congruences Consider the congruence $x^5 \equiv 6 \pmod 7$. We want to find all solutions for $x$.

Step 1: Choose a primitive root and construct an index table.
Modulo $7$, $\phi(7) = 6$. Let us verify $g = 3$ as a primitive root:
  • $3^1 \equiv 3 \pmod 7 \implies \text{ind}_3(3) = 1$
  • $3^2 \equiv 2 \pmod 7 \implies \text{ind}_3(2) = 2$
  • $3^3 \equiv 6 \pmod 7 \implies \text{ind}_3(6) = 3$
  • $3^4 \equiv 4 \pmod 7 \implies \text{ind}_3(4) = 4$
  • $3^5 \equiv 5 \pmod 7 \implies \text{ind}_3(5) = 5$
  • $3^6 \equiv 1 \pmod 7 \implies \text{ind}_3(1) = 0$
Step 2: Translate the congruence into the index domain.
Take the index to base 3 of both sides of $x^5 \equiv 6 \pmod 7$: $$\text{ind}_3(x^5) \equiv \text{ind}_3(6) \pmod{\phi(7)}$$ $$5 \cdot \text{ind}_3(x) \equiv 3 \pmod 6$$ Step 3: Solve the resulting linear congruence.
We now solve $5y \equiv 3 \pmod 6$ where $y = \text{ind}_3(x)$.
Since $\gcd(5, 6) = 1$, there is a unique solution modulo $6$. The modular inverse of $5$ modulo $6$ is $5$ (since $25 \equiv 1 \pmod 6$).
Multiply both sides by $5$: $$25y \equiv 15 \pmod 6 \implies y \equiv 3 \pmod 6$$ Thus, $\text{ind}_3(x) = 3$.

Step 4: Map back to the original group.
Looking at our index table, the element with an index of $3$ is $6$. Therefore, $x \equiv 6 \pmod 7$.

This method generalizes easily. A congruence $x^k \equiv a \pmod n$ has solutions if and only if:

$$\text{ind}_g(a) \text{ is divisible by } \gcd(k, \phi(n))$$

If this divisibility condition holds, there are exactly $\gcd(k, \phi(n))$ distinct solutions modulo $n$. In competitive programming, when $n$ is a prime, finding discrete logarithms efficiently is crucial for solving $x^k \equiv a \pmod n$. In Part D, we will transition from mathematical theory to highly optimized algorithmic implementations, exploring how to compute these indices without constructing an exhaustive $O(n)$ table.
