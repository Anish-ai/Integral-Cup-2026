Part II: Exhaustive Solutions

Solution 1: Polynomial Reducibility

We are asked to factor $x^4 + 1 \pmod{p}$ for all primes $p$. For $p = 2$, $x^4 + 1 = (x^2+1)^2 = (x+1)^4 \pmod{2}$, which is reducible. For $p > 2$, we rewrite $x^4 + 1$ in three algebraically distinct ways by completing the square:

  1. $x^4 + 1 = (x^2 + 1)^2 - 2x^2$
  2. $x^4 + 1 = (x^2 - 1)^2 + 2x^2 = (x^2 - 1)^2 - (-2)x^2$
  3. $x^4 + 1 = (x^2)^2 - (-1)$

For $x^4 + 1$ to be reducible, at least one of these forms must factor as a difference of squares. This requires at least one of $2$, $-2$, or $-1$ to be a quadratic residue modulo $p$.

Recall the multiplicative property of the Legendre symbol: $\left(\frac{-2}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{-1}{p}\right)\left(\frac{2}{p}\right)$.

If $\left(\frac{-1}{p}\right) = 1$ or $\left(\frac{2}{p}\right) = 1$, we are done, as form 3 or form 1 will factor respectively. If both are $-1$, then their product $\left(\frac{-2}{p}\right) = (-1)(-1) = 1$, meaning $-2$ is a quadratic residue, and form 2 will factor. Since the pigeonhole principle guarantees at least one evaluates to $1$, $x^4 + 1$ is reducible modulo every prime $p$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 2: The Sum of Residues

Let $R$ be the set of quadratic residues modulo $p$. We know $|R| = \frac{p-1}{2}$. The sum is $S = \sum_{r \in R} r$.

Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. The quadratic residues are the even powers of $g$: $g^2, g^4, \dots, g^{p-1}$. The sum modulo $p$ forms a geometric progression:

$$ S \equiv \sum_{k=1}^{\frac{p-1}{2}} (g^2)^k = g^2 \frac{(g^2)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} - 1}{g^2 - 1} \pmod{p} $$

Since $(g^2)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = g^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ (by Fermat's Little Theorem), the numerator evaluates to $1 - 1 = 0 \pmod{p}$. Furthermore, since $p > 3$, $g^2 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p}$, meaning the denominator is non-zero in $\mathbb{F}_p$. Thus, $S \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 3: Shifted Character Sum

We evaluate $S = \sum_{x=1}^{p-1} \left(\frac{x(x+a)}{p}\right)$.

Since $x \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$ in the sum, $x$ has a modular inverse $x^{-1}$. We can factor out $x^2$ from the argument:

$$ x(x+a) = x^2(1 + a x^{-1}) $$

Using the multiplicativity of the Legendre symbol, and knowing $\left(\frac{x^2}{p}\right) = 1$:

$$ \left(\frac{x(x+a)}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{x^2}{p}\right) \left(\frac{1 + a x^{-1}}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{1 + a x^{-1}}{p}\right) $$

As $x$ ranges over $1, 2, \dots, p-1$, its inverse $x^{-1}$ also ranges over the exact same set uniquely. Therefore, $y = 1 + a x^{-1}$ ranges over all elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$ exactly once, except for $y = 1$ (since $x^{-1}$ cannot be $0$).

$$ S = \sum_{y \in \mathbb{F}_p, y \ne 1} \left(\frac{y}{p}\right) $$

We know that the sum of Legendre symbols over a complete residue system is $0$ because there are equally many residues as non-residues (Theorem 1.2). Thus, $\sum_{y=0}^{p-1} \left(\frac{y}{p}\right) = 0$.

Our sum $S$ is missing exactly $y=1$. Therefore:

$$ S = 0 - \left(\frac{1}{p}\right) = -1 $$

The sum evaluates precisely to $-1$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 4: Infinitude of 4k+1 Primes

Assume for contradiction there are finitely many primes of the form $4k+1$. Let this complete set be $p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n$.

Construct the number $N = (2 p_1 p_2 \cdots p_n)^2 + 1$. Clearly, $N$ is an odd integer, and $N > 1$. Let $q$ be any prime factor of $N$. Thus, $(2 p_1 \cdots p_n)^2 + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{q}$, which means $(2 p_1 \cdots p_n)^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{q}$.

This implies $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo $q$. By the First Supplementary Law, $\left(\frac{-1}{q}\right) = 1 \iff q \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$.

Therefore, every prime factor $q$ of $N$ must be of the form $4k+1$. However, $N \equiv 1 \pmod{p_i}$ for all $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$, so $q$ cannot be any of the primes in our supposed finite list. This contradicts the assumption that the list was exhaustive. Hence, there are infinitely many primes of the form $4k+1$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 5: Explicit Root via Wilson's Theorem

Wilson's theorem states $(p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. We can expand the factorial:

$$ (p-1)! = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot \dots \cdot \frac{p-1}{2} \cdot \frac{p+1}{2} \cdot \dots \cdot (p-1) $$

Note that the terms in the second half can be rewritten as negative values:

$$ p - k \equiv -k \pmod{p} $$

Substitute this back into the factorial for the upper half:

$$ (p-1)! \equiv \left(1 \cdot 2 \cdots \frac{p-1}{2}\right) \cdot \left(-\frac{p-1}{2} \cdots -2 \cdot -1\right) \pmod{p} $$ $$ (p-1)! \equiv (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \left[ \left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right)! \right]^2 \pmod{p} $$

Since $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, $\frac{p-1}{2}$ is an even integer. Therefore, $(-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = 1$.

$$ -1 \equiv \left[ \left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right)! \right]^2 \pmod{p} $$

Thus, $x = \left(\frac{p-1}{2}\right)! \pmod{p}$ is an explicit integer solution to $x^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 6: The Rule of 3

Using the Law of Quadratic Reciprocity, for an odd prime $p > 3$:

$$ \left(\frac{3}{p}\right) \left(\frac{p}{3}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2} \frac{3-1}{2}} = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} $$

This implies $\left(\frac{3}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \left(\frac{p}{3}\right)$.

We evaluate both terms based on the congruence class of $p$ modulo 4 and modulo 3. Since $p > 3$, $p \equiv 1$ or $2 \pmod{3}$.

  • If $p \equiv 1 \pmod{12}$: $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4} \implies (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = 1$. $p \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \implies \left(\frac{p}{3}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{3}\right) = 1$. Result: $1 \times 1 = 1$.
  • If $p \equiv 5 \pmod{12}$: $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4} \implies (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = 1$. $p \equiv 2 \pmod{3} \implies \left(\frac{2}{3}\right) = -1$. Result: $1 \times -1 = -1$.
  • If $p \equiv 7 \pmod{12}$: $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4} \implies (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = -1$. $p \equiv 1 \pmod{3} \implies \left(\frac{1}{3}\right) = 1$. Result: $-1 \times 1 = -1$.
  • If $p \equiv 11 \pmod{12}$: $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4} \implies (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = -1$. $p \equiv 2 \pmod{3} \implies \left(\frac{2}{3}\right) = -1$. Result: $-1 \times -1 = 1$.

Therefore, 3 is a quadratic residue modulo $p$ if and only if $p \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{12}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 7: Sophie Germain Connection

We need to show $2^p \equiv 1 \pmod{q}$. Since $q = 2p+1$ is prime, we evaluate the Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{2}{q}\right)$ using the Second Supplementary Law.

Since $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, $p$ can be written as $4k+3$. Then $q = 2(4k+3) + 1 = 8k + 7$.

Since $q \equiv 7 \pmod{8}$, the Second Supplementary Law states that $\left(\frac{2}{q}\right) = 1$.

By Euler's Criterion:

$$ \left(\frac{2}{q}\right) \equiv 2^{\frac{q-1}{2}} \pmod{q} $$

Substitute $q - 1 = 2p$, so $\frac{q-1}{2} = p$. Therefore:

$$ 1 \equiv 2^p \pmod{q} $$

Thus, $q$ completely divides $2^p - 1$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 8: Sum of Two Squares Condition

We are given $p \mid (a^2 + b^2)$ and $\gcd(a, b) = 1$. Thus, $a^2 + b^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, which means $a^2 \equiv -b^2 \pmod{p}$.

Since $\gcd(a, b) = 1$, $p$ cannot divide $b$ (if $p \mid b$, then $p \mid a^2$, implying $p \mid a$, contradicting coprimality). Since $b \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, $b$ has an inverse modulo $p$.

Multiply both sides by $(b^{-1})^2$:

$$ (a b^{-1})^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p} $$

This shows that $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$. By the First Supplementary Law, $\left(\frac{-1}{p}\right) = 1 \iff p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 9: Algorithmic Root for 8k+5 Primes

For $p \equiv 5 \pmod{8}$, $p-1 = 4(2k+1)$, meaning the 2-Sylow subgroup has order exactly $4$. Let $a$ be a quadratic residue modulo $p$. Euler's Criterion states $a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$.

Notice that $\frac{p-1}{2} = \frac{p-5}{2} + 2$. Let $v = a^{\frac{p+3}{8}}$. Let's square $v$:

$$ v^2 = a^{\frac{p+3}{4}} = a^{\frac{p-1}{4} + 1} = a \cdot a^{\frac{p-1}{4}} $$

Since $a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = (a^{\frac{p-1}{4}})^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$, the term $a^{\frac{p-1}{4}}$ must be a square root of 1, so it is either $1$ or $-1 \pmod{p}$.

  • Case 1: $a^{\frac{p-1}{4}} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. Then $v^2 \equiv a \pmod{p}$, so $v$ is our square root.
  • Case 2: $a^{\frac{p-1}{4}} \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. Then $v^2 \equiv -a \pmod{p}$. We need a factor of $-1$. Since $p \equiv 5 \pmod{8}$, $\left(\frac{2}{p}\right) = -1$, meaning $2^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. Let $i = 2^{\frac{p-1}{4}}$. Then $i^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. If we multiply $v$ by $i$, $(vi)^2 = v^2 i^2 \equiv (-a)(-1) = a \pmod{p}$. Our root is $v \cdot i \pmod{p}$.
int e(int a, int b, int m) {
    int r = 1;
    a %= m;
    while (b) {
        if (b & 1) r = 1LL * r * a % m;
        a = 1LL * a * a % m;
        b >>= 1;
    }
    return r;
}
int f(int a, int p) {
    int v = e(a, (p + 3) / 8, p);
    int t = 1LL * v * v % p;
    t = 1LL * t * e(a, p - 2, p) % p;
    if (t != 1) {
        int i = e(2, (p - 1) / 4, p);
        v = 1LL * v * i % p;
    }
    return v;
}

Solution 10: Solutions to a Circle

We want to find the number of pairs $(x, y)$ such that $x^2 + y^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. This can be rewritten as $(x - y i)(x + y i) \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$, where $i^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{p}$. The existence of $i$ depends on $p \pmod 4$. We take a simpler geometric approach.

Let $N$ be the number of solutions. Note that $x^2 + y^2 - 1 = 0$. Consider the line intersecting the unit circle at $(1, 0)$ with slope $t \in \mathbb{F}_p$. The line is $y = t(x - 1)$.

Substitute into the circle equation: $x^2 + t^2(x-1)^2 = 1 \implies x^2 - 1 + t^2(x-1)^2 = 0 \implies (x-1)(x+1 + t^2(x-1)) = 0$.

For $x \ne 1$, we divide by $(x-1)$ to get $x+1 + t^2 x - t^2 = 0 \implies x(1+t^2) = t^2 - 1$.

If $1+t^2 \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, there is a unique $x = \frac{t^2-1}{t^2+1}$ and thus a unique $y = \frac{-2t}{t^2+1}$. This gives a bijection between valid slopes $t$ and points on the circle excluding $(1, 0)$.

The number of valid slopes $t$ is the total number of elements in $\mathbb{F}_p$ (which is $p$), minus the roots of $t^2 + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$.

If $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, $t^2 + 1 = 0$ has 2 roots. Valid slopes = $p - 2$. Adding the point $(1,0)$ gives $N = p - 1$.

If $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, $t^2 + 1 = 0$ has 0 roots. Valid slopes = $p$. Adding the point $(1,0)$ gives $N = p + 1$.

Unified mathematically: $N = p - (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 11: Quadratic Form of Modulo 3

Given $a^2 + ab + b^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$ and $\gcd(a, b) = 1$. Multiply the equation by 4:

$$ 4a^2 + 4ab + 4b^2 = (2a + b)^2 + 3b^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{p} $$

This implies $(2a + b)^2 \equiv -3b^2 \pmod{p}$. Since $\gcd(a, b) = 1$, $p \nmid b$. Multiplying by $(b^{-1})^2$ gives:

$$ ((2a+b)b^{-1})^2 \equiv -3 \pmod{p} $$

Thus, $-3$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$. We evaluate $\left(\frac{-3}{p}\right)$ using reciprocity:

$$ \left(\frac{-3}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{-1}{p}\right)\left(\frac{3}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}} \left(\frac{p}{3}\right) = \left(\frac{p}{3}\right) $$

For $\left(\frac{-3}{p}\right)$ to be $1$, we must have $\left(\frac{p}{3}\right) = 1$. The non-zero squares modulo 3 are only $1^2 = 1$ and $2^2 = 1$. Therefore, we must have $p \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 12: Evaluating the Symbol for 5

We evaluate $\left(\frac{5}{p}\right)$ using the Law of Quadratic Reciprocity. Since $5 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, the exponent in reciprocity $\frac{5-1}{2} \cdot \frac{p-1}{2}$ is always even regardless of $p$.

$$ \left(\frac{5}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{p}{5}\right) $$

To find when this is 1, we look at the quadratic residues modulo 5. $\mathbb{Z}_5^* = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$. The squares are $1^2 = 1$, $2^2 = 4 \equiv -1$, $3^2 = 9 \equiv 4$, $4^2 = 16 \equiv 1$.

The quadratic residues modulo 5 are $\{1, 4\} \equiv \{\pm 1\} \pmod{5}$.

Therefore, $\left(\frac{p}{5}\right) = 1 \iff p \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{5}$.

Since $p$ is an odd prime, $p$ must also be odd (i.e., $p \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$). Combining $p \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{5}$ and $p \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$ via the Chinese Remainder Theorem yields $p \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{10}$. $\blacksquare$

Solution 13: Non-Residue Distribution

Let $n > 1$ be the smallest positive integer such that $\left(\frac{n}{p}\right) = -1$. We assume for contradiction that $n$ is composite. Then $n = a \cdot b$ where $1 < a, b < n$.

By the completely multiplicative property of the Legendre symbol:

$$ \left(\frac{n}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) \left(\frac{b}{p}\right) = -1 $$

For the product to be $-1$, exactly one of $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$ or $\left(\frac{b}{p}\right)$ must evaluate to $-1$.

Suppose without loss of generality that $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right) = -1$. Since $a < n$, we have found a smaller positive integer $a$ which is a quadratic non-residue, contradicting the minimality of $n$. Therefore, $n$ cannot be composite and must be a prime. $\blacksquare$

Solution 14: Difference of Squares

We are solving $x^2 - y^2 \equiv a \pmod{p}$. We factor the left side: $(x-y)(x+y) \equiv a \pmod{p}$.

Let $u = x-y$ and $v = x+y$. This is an invertible linear transformation. Since $p$ is odd, for any choice of $u, v \in \mathbb{F}_p$, we can uniquely recover $x$ and $y$ as:

$$ x \equiv (u+v)/2 \pmod{p}, \quad y \equiv (v-u)/2 \pmod{p} $$

The equation simplifies to $u v \equiv a \pmod{p}$. Because $a \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, $u$ cannot be $0$. For every non-zero choice of $u \in \mathbb{F}_p^*$, there exists a unique $v = a u^{-1} \pmod{p}$.

There are exactly $p-1$ choices for $u \in \mathbb{F}_p^*$. Each choice yields a unique $(u, v)$ pair, which maps to a unique $(x, y)$ pair. Therefore, there are exactly $p-1$ solutions. $\blacksquare$

Solution 15: Jacobi Bounding

We consider the group of integers modulo $n$ coprime to $n$, denoted $G = \mathbb{Z}_n^*$. The Solovay-Strassen test checks if $a^{\frac{n-1}{2}} \equiv \left(\frac{a}{n}\right) \pmod{n}$.

Let $H$ be the subset of elements in $G$ for which the congruence holds. It is easy to verify that $H$ is a subgroup of $G$ because both exponentiation and the Jacobi symbol are completely multiplicative. If $x, y \in H$, then $(xy)^{\frac{n-1}{2}} = x^{\frac{n-1}{2}} y^{\frac{n-1}{2}} \equiv \left(\frac{x}{n}\right) \left(\frac{y}{n}\right) = \left(\frac{xy}{n}\right) \pmod{n}$, meaning $xy \in H$.

By Lagrange's Theorem, the size of a subgroup $H$ must divide the size of the group $G$. Therefore, $|H|$ is either equal to $|G|$ (meaning $H = G$) or $|H| \le \frac{1}{2}|G|$.

To prove that at least half the elements fail the test, we only need to show that $H \ne G$. That is, there exists at least one element $w \in G$ (a witness) such that $w^{\frac{n-1}{2}} \not\equiv \left(\frac{w}{n}\right) \pmod{n}$.

Since $n$ is composite, it has a prime factor $p$. Write $n = p^k m$ with $\gcd(p, m) = 1$. If $n$ is not a Carmichael number, finding a witness is trivial via Fermat's Little Theorem. If $n$ is a Carmichael number (meaning $a^{n-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$ for all $a$), it must be square-free, so $n = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_r$ with $r \ge 3$. We can choose an element $w$ such that $\left(\frac{w}{p_1}\right) = -1$ and $w \equiv 1 \pmod{n/p_1}$ using the Chinese Remainder Theorem. Then $\left(\frac{w}{n}\right) = -1$, but $w \equiv 1 \pmod{n/p_1} \implies w^{\frac{n-1}{2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{n/p_1}$. Thus $w^{\frac{n-1}{2}} \not\equiv -1 \pmod{n}$. Since a witness always exists for any composite $n$, $|H| \le \frac{|G|}{2}$. $\blacksquare$
