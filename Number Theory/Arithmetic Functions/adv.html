1. The Linear Sieve for Multiplicative Functions

While the classic Sieve of Eratosthenes operates in $O(n \log \log n)$, evaluating arithmetic functions over large ranges requires strictly $O(n)$ time. This is achieved by ensuring every composite number is visited exactly once—specifically, by its smallest prime factor (SPF).

Theorem 1.1: Unique Factorization by Minimum Prime Every integer $n > 1$ can be uniquely represented as $n = p \cdot k$, where $p$ is the smallest prime factor of $n$, and $p \le \text{SPF}(k)$.
State Transition Logic For a current integer $i$ and prime $p_j$:
1. If $p_j \nmid i$, then $\gcd(i, p_j) = 1$. By multiplicativity, $f(i \cdot p_j) = f(i)f(p_j)$.
2. If $p_j \mid i$, then $p_j$ is the smallest prime factor of $i$. $i$ and $p_j$ share prime factors, so we use function-specific prime power logic:
  • $\phi(i \cdot p_j) = \phi(i) \cdot p_j$
  • $\mu(i \cdot p_j) = 0$ (since $p_j^2$ divides the product)
  • $\tau(i \cdot p_j) = \tau(i) / (e+1) \cdot (e+2)$, where $e$ is the exponent of $p_j$ in $i$.

2. Sublinear Time Execution: Dirichlet Prefix Sum (Mertens Trick)

Evaluating the sum of a multiplicative function $\sum_{i=1}^n f(i)$ when $n \sim 10^{11}$ renders $O(n)$ sieves computationally unviable. The Dirichlet prefix sum trick leverages the ring properties of convolution to compute this in $O(n^{3/4})$ or $O(n^{2/3})$ time.

Theorem 2.1: The Convolution Sum Identity Let $f$ and $g$ be arithmetic functions, and let $S_f(n) = \sum_{i=1}^n f(i)$. Then: $$g(1)S_f(n) = \sum_{i=1}^n (f * g)(i) - \sum_{i=2}^n g(i) S_f\left(\lfloor \frac{n}{i} \rfloor\right)$$
Proof by Interchange of Summation $$\sum_{i=1}^n (f * g)(i) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{d \mid i} g(d)f\left(\frac{i}{d}\right)$$ Setting $i = d \cdot c$, we rewrite the sum over the pairs $(d, c)$ such that $d \cdot c \le n$: $$= \sum_{d=1}^n g(d) \sum_{c=1}^{\lfloor n/d \rfloor} f(c) = \sum_{d=1}^n g(d) S_f\left(\lfloor \frac{n}{d} \rfloor\right)$$ Isolating the $d=1$ term yields: $$\sum_{i=1}^n (f * g)(i) = g(1)S_f(n) + \sum_{d=2}^n g(d) S_f\left(\lfloor \frac{n}{d} \rfloor\right)$$ Rearranging terms provides the recurrence relation. $\blacksquare$
Evaluating Mertens Function in Sublinear Time To find $M(n) = \sum_{i=1}^n \mu(i)$, we choose $f = \mu$ and $g = 1$. We know $(\mu * 1) = \epsilon$, and $\sum_{i=1}^n \epsilon(i) = 1$. The recurrence simplifies to: $$M(n) = 1 - \sum_{i=2}^n M\left(\lfloor \frac{n}{i} \rfloor\right)$$ By precomputing $M(k)$ up to $k = n^{2/3}$ using a linear sieve and memoizing larger states, the complexity drops to $O(n^{2/3})$.

3. Advanced Addition: Min-25 Sieve (Lucy-Hedgehog)

The Min-25 sieve extends the capability to sum multiplicative functions where $f(p)$ is a low-degree polynomial, scaling up to $N = 10^{11}$ in $O(N^{3/4} / \log N)$ or $O(N^{1-\epsilon})$.

Conceptual Framework The algorithm operates in two strictly separated phases:

Phase 1: Sum over Primes. Let $G_k(n)$ be the sum of $f(x)$ for all $x \le n$ such that $x$ is prime OR the minimum prime factor of $x$ is greater than $p_k$. The transitions elegantly strip away composite numbers defined by $p_k$: $$G_k(n) = G_{k-1}(n) - f(p_k) \Big( G_{k-1}\left(\lfloor \frac{n}{p_k} \rfloor\right) - G_{k-1}(p_{k-1}) \Big)$$ Phase 2: Sum over Composites. Using the evaluated prime sums up to $\sqrt{N}$, it recursively integrates the prime powers by leveraging complete multiplicativity, building the total sum bottom-up.
